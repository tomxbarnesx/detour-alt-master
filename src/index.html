DETOUR: LEARN HOW FENTANYL BOTCHED A RUSSIAN HOSTAGE CRISIS

Honduras is a country in Central America with a population of around 9 million people. It gained independence from Spain in 1821 and slowly experienced a similar fate as its Central American neighbors. U.S. companies like the United Fruit Company — later Chiquita — moved into Honduras with big tax exemptions and land handouts.

They exported Honduran bananas but contributed little to the growth of the country’s economy.

The term “banana republic”, used to describe a country that relies on one main export like bananas and is usually exploited for its resources by foreign interests, was first used in 1904 by the writer O. Henry to describe a fictionalized version of Honduras.

"The consul was interested in his report. He was only twenty-four; and he had not been in Coralio long enough for his enthusiasm to cool in the heat of the tropics—a paradox that may be allowed between Cancer and Capricorn.

"So many thousand bunches of bananas, so many thousand oranges and cocoanuts, so many ounces of gold dust, pounds of rubber, coffee, indigo and sarsaparilla—actually, exports were twenty per cent. Greater than for the previous year!"

— From O. Henry’s Cabbages and Kings
